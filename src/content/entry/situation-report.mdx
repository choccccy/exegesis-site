Delta site is set up around a thirteen-kilometer-wide hole near the equator of the Slumbering Empyrean, identified even before Nysa's Wake completed its final insertion burn in the EK Draconis system; much larger holes exist, but few match its apparently bottomless depth.

This becomes stranger with initial probe exploration, before crewed expeditions are greenlit by the Antikythera, which finds a gravitational anomaly within the abyss. For the first hundred kilometers there is minimal effect, but as probes travel deeper they are eventually unable to escape what appears to be an event horizon in the narrowing chasm, at an estimated 1,400 kilometers below the surface of the Empyrean. Even if they are not destroyed by the crushing gravity, primion excitation increases with depth into the hole, preventing exploration by even the simplest of drones by a depth of 350 kilometers.

Probes that are not lost to this anomaly report that the walls of the hole resemble a cross section of the Slumbering Empyrean, showcasing hundreds of distinct structures and thousands of openings spanning from one side of the chasm to the other. Exploration of most of these tunnels just find dead ends, while others appear to serve some sort of circulatory function: most spout water into the void, but others occasionally dislodge other materials.

One particularly notable pipe occasionally spits a variable number of cobalt spheres into the chasm, which contain between them an atmosphere of krypton at several gigapascals. Another ejects a glowing, white-hot ferrous fluid at 2,482 °C, magnetically contained as it floats across the hole into a receiving opening. Yet another, nearly seven hundred meters wide, features a cloud of dodecahedral diamonds with faces roughly forty-seven centimeters to a side, traveling across the chasm at several dozen kilometers per second into a receiving hole. A different opening, just a few centimeters wide, disgorges a beam of protons at 99.9999999994% of the speed of light, which intersects the diamond cloud; diamonds struck by this beam are mostly annihilated. Hundreds of tubes emit trace gases and significant primion radiation.

Openings on the opposite side of the hole from an outputting tunnel seem safer, although many occasionally 'back up' and have their own, less frequent ejections. Yet more tunnels have no evident function and are the primary candidates for exploration.
